    • Chembra Peak

    Chembra peak is one of the risky tourist endeavours and is the highest peak in Wayanad at 2100m, above the Mean Sea Level.

    It is a mountain in the Western Ghats of Kerala, India. It's known for its lush greenery, diverse flora and fauna, and stunning views of the surrounding valleys.

    Its the highest peak in the Wayanad hills and one of the highest peaks in the Western Ghats, adjoining the Nilgiri Hills and Vellarimala.

    At the top of Chembra, there is a fresh water lake called 'Hridayasarassu' in the shape of heart. The blue-eyed water in the lake never dries up even in the peak of summer.

    The scenic beauty of Wayanad which is visible from the top of Chembra is very challenging and thrilling. All along the steep and slippery way to the top of the hill, the whispering of the flowing spring which sprouts from the top of the hill accompanies the tourist.

    Banasura Sagar Dam

    Banasura Sagar Dam is the largest earth dam in India and second largest in Asia. It's built across the Karamanathodu River, a tributary of the Kabini River. The Banasura project precincts are an ideal starting point for treks to the Banasura Peak. An interesting feature is a set of islands that were formed when the reservoir submerged the surrounding areas. This large expanse of water is bounded on one side with magnificent hills of heavenly charm. The dam offers breathtaking views of the Banasura mountains and picturesque surroundings. The reservoir is a popular tourist attraction. Visitors to Banasura Sagar Dam can indulge in various activities such as boating, trekking, and nature walks. There are also options for speed boating and pedal boating on the reservoir.

    En Ooru

    A village reflects the indigenous culture and traditional lifestyle of the Ethnic Community of the place. En Ooru, the first tribal heritage village in Kerala, introduce indigenous architecture coupled with tribal customs and traditions. Opened at Pookode in Wayanad district by the State Government, En Ooru is spread across 25 acres of land on the slope of a hillock.

    En Ooru Tribal Heritage Village is an initiative of the Government of Kerala to create an exclusive and permanent platform for the heritage & indigenous skills of the tribes domiciled in Kerala. The Government of Kerala is on a mission to transform this model tribal village into a microcosm of the tribes of Kerala.

    Pazhassi Raja Tomb

    Veera Pazhassi Raja, known as the Lion of Kerala, epitomizes the spirit of resistance against British colonial rule in the annals of Kerala's history. Born into a lineage steeped in valour and leadership, Raja's early years were marked by tales of bravery and determination, laying the groundwork for his pivotal role in the Pazhassi Revolt. Rising to prominence as a beacon of defiance, he rallied his people with strategic brilliance, employing guerrilla warfare tactics amidst the dense forests of Wayanad to thwart British dominance. His alliance with tribal communities not only strengthened his forces but also integrated their indigenous knowledge, proving instrumental in sustaining the resistance against the colonial oppressors.

    The memorial of The Lion of Kerala Veera Pazhassi Raja, who organised the Guerrilla Warfare against the British East India Company, is situated at Mananthavady.

    Thirunelli Temple boasts architectural marvels that reflect the mastery of traditional Kerala craftsmanship. Moreover, the temple's location amidst the untamed beauty of Wayanad's natural landscapes adds to its allure, offering pilgrims a journey of spiritual renewal amidst the serene embrace of forested hills and babbling brooks.     Pakshipathalam

    Pakshipathalam is nestled amidst the lush greenery of the Western Ghats and is renowned for its birdlife, particularly its population of indigenous and migratory birds. The name "Pakshipathalam" translates to "Abode of Birds" in the local language. It's a favorite spot for nature lovers, birdwatchers, and adventure enthusiasts. It is in Bramha Giri hills at Thirunelly and is a challenging tourist spot. It is 7 kms. north-east of Thirunelly temple and is situated 1740 m. above mean sea level. The region is also known for its rich biodiversity and stunning landscapes. To reach ‘Pakshipathalam’, 17 kms. have to be covered through wild forest. The deep rock caves formed among the thick blocks of rocks at the northern top end of the Brahmagiri is the abode of various birds and wild beasts. It is a formation of large boulders, some as tall as two storey buildings. The deep caves found here are home to a wide variety of birds, animals and distinctive species of plants. One of the unique features of Pakshipathalam is its cave system. Exploring these caves is a thrilling experience for visitors.

    Jain Temple

    Bathery Jain Temple Wayanad was constructed in the 13th century CE during the rule of the Vijayanagara Empire. It served as an important Jain center and a shrine during the peak of Jainism in Wayanad. Then, when Shaivism and Vaishnavism of the Hindu religion flourished, it became an important center for their activities.

    In the 18th century, when Tipu Sultan ruled the Kingdom of Mysore, he used this Jain Temple in Wayanad as the battery (ammunition store). The temple also housed some of his armies.

    The Jain Temple in Wayanad is one of the holiest sites for Jain followers in the region. It’s one of the most important medieval Jain settlements in Wayanad and around. This temple is also one of the oldest Jain temples in Kerala.

    It once served an array of purposes apart from being a religious shrine. It was also sheltering Tipu Sultan’s army in the 18th century. Today, only the temple structure remains. And it stands tall with its beams and pillars.

    This ruined Jain Temple in Wayanad is an educational and historical destination. It’s known as the Ancient Jain Temple. And it’s a symbol of the rich cultural legacy of Kerala.

    The Kuruva island

    Kuruva Island (also known as Kuruva Dweep) is a renowned eco-tourism destination located in the Wayanad district of Kerala, India. It is a protected river delta of ever green forest spread across 950 acres on the tributaries of east flowing river Kabini which is a tributary of the Cauvery River. The island is famous for its rich biodiversity and serene natural beauty, making it a popular spot for nature lovers and adventure enthusiasts. The island is home to a variety of flora and fauna, including rare species of birds, orchids, and herbs. Its dense forests, rivers, and streams provide a unique habitat that supports diverse wildlife.

    Tourist Attractions and Activities

    1. Nature Walks: The island offers peaceful walks through dense bamboo groves, and you can explore various species of flora and fauna. The trails are usually accompanied by the soothing sounds of nature, making for a tranquil experience.

    2. Bird Watching: Kuruva Island is a haven for bird watchers, with many migratory and resident birds frequenting the area. You can spot species such as the Malabar Grey Hornbill, Scarlet Minivet, and several types of kingfishers.

    3. Bamboo Rafting: One of the most popular activities on Kuruva Island is bamboo rafting. Visitors can enjoy a serene ride on the river, providing a unique perspective of the surrounding forest and river ecosystem.

    4. Picnicking: The island’s natural beauty makes it an ideal spot for picnics. There are designated areas where visitors can relax and enjoy a meal amidst nature.

    5. Trekking: Though the island is relatively flat, there are some trekking opportunities along marked trails that provide a good way to explore the island's interior.

    Cultural and Environmental Importance

    Kuruva Island is not just a tourist spot but also an important ecological and cultural heritage site. It plays a significant role in maintaining the local biodiversity and providing a natural sanctuary for wildlife. The island’s environment is crucial for the local communities and supports various ecological functions, including water purification and habitat preservation.

    Conservation Efforts

    The local authorities, along with environmental organizations, are actively involved in conservation efforts to protect Kuruva Island’s delicate ecosystem. Visitors are encouraged to be mindful of their impact and to support initiatives that promote sustainable tourism.

    Kuruva Island offers a unique and enriching experience for visitors, blending natural beauty with opportunities for adventure and relaxation. Whether you are an avid nature lover, a bird-watching enthusiast, or simply looking for a peaceful retreat, Kuruva Island provides a perfect escape into the heart of nature.

    Phantom Rock

    Phantom Rock is a unique and popular tourist attraction located near Ambalavayal in the Wayanad district of Kerala, India. Known for its distinctive shape, which resembles a human skull or the head of a phantom, the rock formation has captured the fascination of many visitors. The immediate surroundings offer excellent photo opportunities.

    Geographical and Geological Overview

    Elevation: The rock is perched at an elevation of around 2,600 feet above sea level, providing stunning views of the surrounding landscape.

    Formation: The rock is a natural geological formation primarily composed of metamorphic rocks like granite and gneiss. Erosion and weathering over time have given it its characteristic skull-like appearance.

    Attractions and Activities

    1. Scenic Views: Phantom Rock offers panoramic views of the surrounding hills, valleys, and forests of Wayanad. The vantage point from the rock provides excellent opportunities for photography and sightseeing.

    2. Trekking: The area around Phantom Rock is popular for trekking. The hike to the rock involves traversing through rocky terrains, forested areas, and open fields. It is a relatively easy trek, suitable for beginners and families.

    3. Photography: The unique shape of Phantom Rock and the picturesque surroundings make it a favorite spot for photographers. The best time for capturing stunning shots is during sunrise or sunset when the light casts dramatic shadows on the rock formation.

    4. Nature Exploration: The area around Phantom Rock is rich in flora and fauna. Visitors can explore the local vegetation, observe various bird species, and enjoy the natural beauty of Wayanad.

    5. Cultural Experience: The rock is close to local tribal settlements and traditional villages, providing an opportunity to learn about the culture and lifestyle of the indigenous people of Wayanad.

    Environmental and Cultural Significance

    Phantom Rock holds a significant place in the local tourism landscape of Wayanad. It not only attracts tourists with its unique natural formation but also serves as a symbol of the region's rich geological and ecological diversity. The rock formation and its surroundings are part of the Western Ghats, a UNESCO World Heritage site, known for its biodiversity and cultural importance.

    Conservation Efforts

    As a natural landmark, Phantom Rock is subject to conservation efforts aimed at preserving its unique geological features and the surrounding ecosystem. Visitors are encouraged to follow eco-friendly practices, such as avoiding littering and respecting the natural environment.

    Edakkal Caves

    Edakkal Caves are a significant archaeological site located in the Wayanad district of Kerala, India. These caves are renowned for their ancient petroglyphs, which offer a glimpse into the prehistoric human activity and the cultural history of the region.

    Geographical and Historical Context

    • Location: Edakkal Caves are situated on the Ambukuthi Hill, approximately 25 km from Kalpetta and about 4 km from the town of Ambalavayal.

    • Elevation: The caves are located at an elevation of around 1,200 meters (3,937 feet) above sea level. Discovery: The caves were discovered by Fred Fawcett, a British police official, in 1894 while on a hunting expedition.

    Cave Structure and Significance

    • Formation: The name "Edakkal" means "a stone in between," which aptly describes the formation. The caves are formed by a large split in the rock, creating a natural shelter.

    • Two Chambers: The caves consist of two main chambers. The lower chamber is about 18 feet long, 12 feet wide, and 10 feet high. The upper chamber is larger, measuring about 96 feet long, 22 feet wide, and 18 feet high.

    • Petroglyphs: The walls of the caves are adorned with carvings and drawings that date back to the Neolithic age, estimated to be between 6,000 and 8,000 years old. The petroglyphs depict human figures, animals, and geometric shapes, showcasing the artistic and cultural expressions of early inhabitants.

      • Significant Findings

      1. Ancient Carvings: The carvings in Edakkal Caves are among the oldest known petroglyphs in India. They include representations of human and animal figures, symbols, and scripts that suggest connections to ancient civilizations.

      2. Historical Importance: The carvings provide valuable insights into the life, beliefs, and social practices of prehistoric humans. Some symbols resemble those found in the Indus Valley Civilization, hinting at possible cultural exchanges.
